Understanding and Stopping Gender Discrimination

What is Gender Inequality?

Gender inequality is discrimination on the basis of sex or gender causing one sex or gender to be routinely privileged or prioritized over another.

Gender equality is a fundamental human right and that right is violated by gender-based discrimination. Gender disparity starts in childhood and is right now limiting the lifelong potential of children around the world – disproportionately affecting girls.

Around the world, while contexts and gender roles vary from place to place, we can see that gender inequalities occur everywhere; and at every stage of life, beginning with childhood or even before birth.

Why is it important today?

Gender discrimination is the unequal treatment of people based on their gender. That includes granting privileges to a certain gender or marginalizing someone because of their gender identity. Unequal pay, sexual harassment, and restricted or eliminated access to rights like education and healthcare are forms of gender discrimination. On an individual level, stopping gender discrimination requires internal reflection and willingness to change. As the data makes clear, however, gender discrimination is a systemic issue. The World Economic Forum estimates it will take 136 years to reach gender equality.

Key Ways to End Discrimination

1. Empower Women in the Workplace

In most workplaces, there are more men than women in high-level positions. The gender pay gap also persists in many places and intersects with race and ethnicity. Gender discrimination can be tackled by offering paid leave and childcare, supporting more women in senior roles, and reviewing salaries. It should also be remembered that the Covid-19 pandemic had a big effect on workplace gender equality. More women than men left their jobs, often because women tend to do more childcare.

2. Workplace Equality

One way to promote fairness is ensuring women have opportunities for leadership roles and advancement in the workplace. Employers can implement policies like paid family leave, affordable childcare, and reviewing hiring and compensation practices to reduce gender gaps. Fostering more representation of women in senior positions is also important.

3. Strengthening Legal Protections

Discrimination sometimes enables violations like harassment, assault, and domestic violence. Having robust anti-discrimination laws sends a message and allows for legal recourse. Enforcing protections, including those for economic inclusion, with the full force of the law is crucial. Recent events have shown the ongoing need to review and strengthen these laws as needed.

4. Taking a Multi-Pronged Approach

A coordinated effort is necessary to drive real, lasting change as no single solution alone can end deeply entrenched discrimination. Tackling these issues through initiatives in workplaces, policies, healthcare, and social attitudes in parallel is important for making meaningful progress toward true gender parity.

5. Reproductive Health and Rights

Limited access to reproductive healthcare disproportionately impacts certain groups. Providing modern contraception options and maternal care helps both women's health and socioeconomic empowerment. Protecting these rights, especially for young people, helps remove barriers to education and career development.
